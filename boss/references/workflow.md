# Agency Workflow

## Mandatory Role Sequence

Use this sequence for brand cases and integrated campaigns:

1. AE collects client needs with the brief template.
2. AE drafts the problem and objectives from the collected needs.
3. AE, Strategy Director, and Creative Director jointly confirm the problem and objectives.
4. AE identifies and gathers the materials needed to solve the problem.
5. Strategy Director creates the strategy from the confirmed objectives and materials.
6. Creative Director creates creative directions from the strategy.
7. AE, Strategy Director, and Creative Director jointly confirm the creative direction.
8. Copywriter and Designer create concrete content from the confirmed direction and requirements.

Do not skip stages. If a later role lacks required input, return to the relevant previous stage.
Do not move to the next stage until the current role output is explicitly confirmed by the user.
If any required material is missing, explicitly ask the user for it before continuing.

## Stage Gates

### 1. AE Brief Intake

Required output:

- Internal brief
- Known information
- Missing information
- Questions to confirm

Gate: Do not proceed until the problem and objectives can be stated clearly or assumptions are explicitly marked.
User gate: submit the intake summary to the user before moving forward.
Missing-material gate: ask the user for any missing client, brand, product, audience, or scope information before moving forward.

### 2. Problem and Objective Alignment

Required output:

- Draft problem statement
- Draft objective statement
- Core problem
- Business objective
- Communication objective
- Target audience
- Success criteria
- Constraints and risks

Gate: Do not ask Strategy Director for strategy until AE, Strategy Director, and Creative Director explicitly confirm the problem and objectives.
User gate: submit the problem and objective output to the user before moving forward.
Missing-material gate: ask the user for any unresolved business, audience, or objective inputs before moving forward.

### 3. AE Research and Material Collection

Required output:

- Industry research needed
- Competitor materials needed
- Consumer or audience materials needed
- Brand/product materials needed
- Channel and media context needed
- Historical campaign or performance materials needed
- Materials already available
- Materials still missing

Gate: Do not finalize strategy if key materials are missing; Strategy may only create hypotheses and data requests.
User gate: submit the research request and material status to the user before moving forward.
Missing-material gate: explicitly ask the user for missing research, competitor, consumer, brand, product, or channel materials before moving forward.

### 4. Strategy Development

Required output:

- Problem diagnosis
- Audience insight
- Category and competitor judgment
- Core proposition
- Communication task
- Campaign strategy
- Channel roles
- Creative mandatories

Gate: Do not ask Creative Director for final creative directions until the strategy is clear enough to guide creative choices.
User gate: submit the strategy output to the user before moving forward.
Missing-material gate: ask the user for missing evidence, proof points, or positioning inputs before moving forward.

### 5. Creative Direction

Required output:

- 2-3 creative directions
- Direction name
- Core creative idea
- Creative mechanism
- Key message expression
- Possible visual and content extensions
- Risks and tradeoffs

Gate: Do not brief Copywriter or Designer until AE, Strategy Director, and Creative Director confirm one direction or a clear route hierarchy.
User gate: submit the creative direction output to the user before moving forward.
Missing-material gate: ask the user for any missing mandatories, constraints, or approval conditions before moving forward.

### 6. Three-party Direction Confirmation

AE confirms:

- The direction answers the client need and respects project constraints.
- Scope, timeline, and deliverables are clear enough for execution.

Strategy Director confirms:

- The direction fits the problem, objective, audience insight, and core proposition.

Creative Director confirms:

- The direction has creative strength, extension potential, and execution feasibility.

Required output:

- Confirmed creative direction
- Reasons for selection
- Rejected or backup directions
- Execution boundaries
- Copy task list
- Design task list

User gate: submit the confirmed direction package to the user before starting copy or design execution.

### 7. Copy and Design Execution

Copywriter creates:

- Campaign theme
- KV copy
- Social copy
- Video scripts
- Naming or content lines

Designer creates:

- Visual direction
- KV brief
- Layout and information hierarchy
- Material adaptation
- AI image or design prompts

Gate: Creative Director reviews execution against the confirmed strategy and creative direction.
User gate: submit Copywriter output and Designer output to the user for confirmation before final review, packaging, or next-step integration.
Missing-material gate: ask the user for any missing copy mandatories, brand assets, packaging specs, size specs, or platform constraints before finalizing outputs.

## Proposal Skeleton

- Cover
- Project understanding
- Challenge
- Audience insight
- Strategy
- Creative idea
- Key visual direction
- Campaign architecture
- Channel/content plan
- Sample executions
- Timeline
- Next steps

## Deck Completeness Rules

When generating a deck, optimize for narrative completeness and decision quality, not artificial slide-count restraint.

- Do not compress a proposal into too few slides if that causes strategic gaps, weak transitions, missing proof, or underdeveloped execution examples.
- Expand the deck when needed to fully explain the problem, insight, strategy, creative idea, visual system, campaign mechanics, sample executions, rollout logic, and next steps.
- Treat the `Proposal Skeleton` as a minimum structural baseline, not a hard page limit.
- Add extra slides when a section needs clearer logic, stronger selling, richer examples, or cleaner separation between ideas.
- Prefer one clear idea per slide over overcrowded slides that try to do too much at once.
- If the deck becomes longer, keep the rhythm intentional: each added slide must earn its place by improving clarity, persuasion, or completeness.
- Do not cut necessary slides only to appear concise. Concision is good; incompleteness is not.
